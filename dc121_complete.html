Doctrine & Covenants 121

Liberty Jail Revelation on God's Compassion and Priesthood Power
March 20, 1839 • Liberty Jail, Clay County, Missouri
"No power or influence can or ought to be maintained by virtue of the priesthood, only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned."
— D&C 121:41

D&C 121: Section Overview

Liberty Jail Revelation on God's Compassion and Priesthood Power

Basic Information


Date of Revelation: March 20, 1839
Location: Liberty Jail, Clay County, Missouri
Recipients: Joseph Smith, Hyrum Smith, Sidney Rigdon, Lyman Wight, Alexander McRae, Caleb Baldwin, and the scattered Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
Section Summary: During the Saints' fiercest persecution, Joseph Smith cried out from Liberty Jail for deliverance. The Lord answered with assurance of His awareness, promised eventual justice, unfolded expansive teachings on the nature of priesthood power, and offered a heavenly perspective on adversity and divine timing.

This revelation captures the transition from pleading lament (verses 1-6) to a divine epistolary response (verses 7-46). The Lord acknowledges suffering, reaffirms the future gathering of promised knowledge, and codifies the governing principles of righteous priesthood leadership—persuasion, gentleness, meekness, and love unfeigned. These teachings continue to define Latter-day Saint leadership, resilience, and hope.

Key Principles


Primary Doctrinal Principle: Priesthood power is inseparably connected to the powers of heaven and is exercised only through righteousness, charity, and pure motives (D&C 121:34-46).
Secondary Principle: God hears the anguished petitions of His covenant people and, in His time, delivers, sustains, and exalts them beyond their present afflictions (D&C 121:7-8).
Application Principle: Faithful Saints discipline their hearts, reject unrighteous dominion, and cultivate Christlike attributes so heaven can pour down knowledge and favor upon them (verses 26-45).

Timeframe & Setting

Circumstances Among Recipients

Joseph Smith and his companions had endured nearly four months of winter confinement in Liberty Jail. The dungeon-like lower room measured roughly 14 by 14 feet with a 6½-foot ceiling, little natural light, and constant damp chill. They survived on sparse, sometimes poisoned food and suffered physical illness. Joseph's correspondence reveals alternating anguish, yearning, and prophetic confidence. The Saints outside were scattered across blizzards, forced from their homes, and desperate for direction. Joseph's immediate audience therefore consisted of exhausted men striving to sustain one another while pleading for divine aid.

Circumstances Within the Church

By March 1839, the Church had been legally and militarily expelled from Missouri under Governor Lilburn Boggs's extermination order. Many leaders were displaced; the Twelve Apostles had been commanded to depart on missions from Far West but were in hiding. The Saints were migrating toward Quincy, Illinois, where residents offered temporary refuge. Organizational structures were fragile: quorum meetings were sporadic, communication was perilous, and morale was severely tested. Joseph's words needed to steady leadership, reaffirm divine oversight, and prevent widespread apostasy.

Relevant Local Circumstances

Clay County officials held Joseph and his companions on charges of treason after a series of legally questionable court-martials. Local militias patrolled the area, mobs guarded the jail, and civil authority often colluded with vigilante violence. The Liberty Jail letter responded to a climate of intimidation: property confiscation, incarcerations without due process, and an economy disrupted by forced evacuations. The jail itself sat within a community divided over Mormon presence, amplifying tension and fear for both prisoners and their families.

Relevant Global Circumstances

The world of 1839 wrestled with revolutions, religious revivals, and contested notions of liberty. The United States grappled with slavery expansion, Native American displacement, and debates over states' rights. Globally, monarchies negotiated the aftermath of the Napoleonic era while industrialization accelerated social change. For the Latter-day Saints, the persecution in Missouri became a case study in religious discrimination during a century that championed Enlightenment ideals yet often denied them in practice. The Liberty Jail revelations therefore speak to universal struggles for conscience, governance, and divine justice.

Purpose of the Revelation

Short-Term Purpose

  • Comfort Joseph Smith and fellow prisoners amid hopeless circumstance.
  • Reassure scattered Saints that the Lord remained aware and guiding events.
  • Command patience, forgiveness, and hope while legal appeals stalled.
  • Clarify why relief had not yet come and promise eventual vindication.
  • Give actionable counsel for sustaining priesthood leadership despite incarceration.

Long-Term Purpose

  • Codify the eternal law governing priesthood authority and stewardship.
  • Teach generations of Saints how adversity refines leaders and unlocks revelation.
  • Establish a doctrine of righteous governance contrasted with worldly dominion.
  • Promise future dispensations of knowledge, empowering the Restoration's momentum.
  • Anchor Latter-day Saint theology in a God who transforms suffering into sanctifying experience.

Significance

Immediate Significance

  • Reframed the Liberty Jail ordeal from senseless persecution to a sanctifying "small moment" preparing Joseph for greater service.
  • Unified displaced Saints through published excerpts that circulated in Far West, Quincy, and Nauvoo.
  • Neutralized apostate narratives by affirming Joseph's divine appointment and prophetic insight.
  • Provided a doctrinal charter for how church leaders should minister under duress.

Enduring Significance

  • D&C 121 remains the Restoration's most comprehensive treatise on priesthood character, widely cited in modern general conference addresses.
  • The revelation undergirds contemporary policies on ministering, discipline, and leadership development.
  • It models faithful lament—honest petitions that transition into trust, a pattern adopted in personal and institutional crises.
  • The prophetic promise that knowledge will "distil as the dews from heaven" fuels modern temple work, continuing revelation, and global outreach.

Cultural Insights

Ancient Context

Joseph's language invokes biblical lament tradition. Phrases like "O God, where art thou?" echo Psalm 10:1 and Psalm 13:1-2, wherein righteous sufferers appeal to God's justice. References to "the pavilion" and "hiding place" mirror imagery from Psalm 27:5 and Psalm 31:20, signaling Joseph's scriptural literacy. The Lord's response engages apocalyptic motifs—"times and seasons," "thrones and dominions"—linking Liberty Jail with ancient prophetic assurances that heavens remain ordered even when earth descends into chaos.

Modern Parallels

Modern Saints confront institutional distrust, power abuses, and societal polarization. D&C 121 equips believers to resist authoritarian impulses, promote compassionate leadership, and advocate for religious liberty. The Liberty Jail narrative parallels contemporary stories of unjust incarceration, displacement, and refugees seeking safe haven. By highlighting divine empathy and covenant accountability, the revelation informs humanitarian advocacy and conflict transformation in the twenty-first century.

Key Doctrines

  • Priesthood Stewardship: Authority is valid only when exercised in alignment with heaven's principles (verses 34-46).
  • Divine Empathy: God hears and responds to the heartfelt cries of His servants, even when outward deliverance tarries (verses 1-11).
  • Justice and Mercy: The wicked who persecute Zion will face equitable judgment; God's timing preserves agency while ensuring accountability (verses 12-25).
  • Revelatory Abundance: The dispensation of the fulness of times opens reservoirs of knowledge promised to ancient prophets (verses 26-33).
  • Sanctifying Opposition: Trials refine rather than destroy when endured with faith—echoing Christ's descent below all things (verse 7 and D&C 122:8).
  • Charity as Binding Power: Charity toward all and virtue-garnished thoughts elevate confidence before God and secure lasting dominion (verses 45-46).
  • Nature of Leadership: Righteous governance rejects compulsion, functioning instead through kindness, pure knowledge, and unfeigned love (verses 41-42).

D&C 121: Historical Context

Liberty Jail, Missouri – December 1838 to April 1839

1. Historical Setting

The winter of 1838-1839 marked the nadir of the Latter-day Saint experience in Missouri. Following the escalation of the "Mormon War" in late October 1838, Governor Lilburn W. Boggs issued Executive Order 44—the infamous extermination order—authorizing state militia to expel or exterminate the Saints. Days later, the Haun's Mill massacre left 17 Saints dead, intensifying the sense of crisis. Joseph Smith and other leaders surrendered at Far West on October 31 under promises of safety that were swiftly broken. They were subjected to a court-martial in Richmond without legal counsel and charged with treason, murder, and arson.

Liberty Jail became the holding site for Joseph Smith, Hyrum Smith, Sidney Rigdon, Lyman Wight, Alexander McRae, and Caleb Baldwin beginning December 1, 1838. The jail—constructed of double walls with a rubble infill, creating an 8-foot-thick barrier—was intended to prevent escape by both physical and psychological intimidation. Prisoners were confined in the lower chamber, accessible only via a trapdoor. The ceiling allowed little room to stand, and light filtered through two small grated windows. Winter winds penetrated the cracks, keeping the room perpetually cold. In Joseph's words, the space was "as dark almost as midnight." Food brought by guards was frequently poisoned or contaminated; Joseph once vomited violently after tasting a meal and then forbade the others from eating.

While incarcerated, Joseph drafted several letters to the Saints. The March 20, 1839 missive—later canonized as D&C 121-123—combined petition, doctrinal exposition, and administrative instruction. Contextually, the revelation served as a spiritual turning point: Joseph reframed the crisis from unmitigated tragedy to divinely supervised probation. By couching his lament in biblical language, he aligned the Saints' suffering with Israel's covenant history.

Beyond the jail, thousands of Saints evacuated in midwinter across the frozen Mississippi River into Quincy, Illinois. Local residents welcomed them with supplies, shelter, and legal advocacy, contrasting sharply with Missouri's hostility. The exodus strained families; women and children traveled with limited food, encountering sickness and exposure. News of Joseph's imprisonment intensified feelings of abandonment and uncertainty. The Liberty Jail letters therefore functioned as a lifeline between prophet and people, offering theological explanations and practical directives.

2. Key Figures

Joseph Smith Jr.

At 33, Joseph faced spiritual isolation yet displayed prophetic clarity. His letters reveal repeated efforts to comfort the Saints, instruct leaders, and petition government officials. The Liberty Jail period crystallized Joseph's theology of suffering and priesthood governance. Despite physical weakness, he mentored fellow prisoners, led communal prayer, and bore testimony of divine oversight. His ability to transform anguish into revelation underscores his prophetic calling.

Hyrum Smith

Joseph's older brother and trusted counselor provided emotional steadiness. Hyrum's letters to his family emphasized patience and faith. He served as a spiritual anchor inside the jail, reinforcing the message that God would vindicate them. Hyrum's presence also manifested the familial cost of persecution—Emma Smith and Mary Fielding Smith shouldered parallel burdens of displacement and caretaking.

Sidney Rigdon

A counselor in the First Presidency, Rigdon endured severe illness during imprisonment. His health deteriorated to the point that guards eventually allowed him to seek bail. His rhetorical skills and legal knowledge had earlier aided the Church, but in Liberty Jail he grappled with depression, foreshadowing later struggles. D&C 121 indirectly addresses leaders like Rigdon by outlining how priesthood influence must adhere to heavenly law.

Lyman Wight, Alexander McRae, Caleb Baldwin

These leaders represented the broader leadership body. Wight, an outspoken defender of the Saints, provided detailed journal accounts of jail conditions. McRae and Baldwin's testimonies later aided Joseph's legal appeals. Their participation in the revelation's reception confirms that the Lord addressed the entire leadership quorum, not Joseph alone.

Emma Hale Smith

Outside the prison, Emma executed an arduous balancing act: caring for children, managing property seizures, and advocating for incarcerated loved ones. Her letters to Joseph conveyed updates and yearning, while she navigated hostile mobs and scarce resources. Emma traveled to Liberty in January 1839, braving winter storms to spend brief visits through the iron grates. Her resilience symbolized the sacrifice of Latter-day Saint women.

Governor Lilburn W. Boggs & General John B. Clark

As architects of the extermination order, Boggs and Clark personified civil betrayal. Their decisions legitimized mob violence and signaled that state protection was unavailable. D&C 121's condemnation of those who "lift up the heel against mine anointed" speaks directly to officials who weaponized governmental power to oppress religious minorities.

3. Cultural and Religious Context

The United States in the 1830s experienced the Second Great Awakening's fervor: camp meetings, itinerant preachers, and theological experimentation proliferated. Americans wrestled with questions about authority, scripture, and communal structures. Latter-day Saints, with claims of new scripture and modern prophets, elicited fascination and fear. Their rapid growth in Missouri threatened established religious communities who perceived the Saints as economic and political rivals.

Anti-Mormon literature circulated widely. Newspapers like the Missouri Argus depicted the Saints as fanatics seeking domination. Religious pluralism in theory clashed with sectarian competition in practice. Liberty Jail thus epitomized the broader struggle for religious freedom: the Saints appealed to constitutional ideals, yet local prejudice often overruled legal protections.

The cultural hierarchy in frontier Missouri privileged older settlers and slaveholding elites. Latter-day Saints, many from northern states and opposition to slavery, clashed with pro-slavery sentiments. Their bloc voting behavior intensified accusations that they intended to control local elections. Religious rhetoric merged with political fear-mongering, culminating in violent vigilantism masked as community defense.

Within the Church, the Kirtland apostasy of 1837-1838 had already shaken confidence. Some former leaders—including David Whitmer and John Whitmer—publicly opposed Joseph. D&C 121's teachings about unrighteous dominion respond to these internal fractures, warning that aspiring to the "honors of men" disqualifies individuals from divine trust. The revelation reasserted that priesthood legitimacy rests on celestial rather than popular approval.

4. Political and Social Dynamics

Missouri's political climate was volatile. Frontier justice, vigilante committees, and militia interventions blurred lines between legal authority and mob rule. Local leaders like Boggs exploited anti-Mormon sentiment for political gain, portraying the Saints as insurrectionists. Courts showed bias; grand juries indicted Joseph on dubious evidence, while crimes against Saints rarely received prosecution. Property confiscations, burning of farms, and threats of extermination became socially accepted tools of suppression.

Social stratification intensified conflict. Many Missourians feared economic displacement as Latter-day Saints purchased land and established thriving communities. The communal nature of Mormon migration—distinct speech, dress, worship, and cooperative labor—fueled xenophobic reactions. The panic of 1837 left the Missouri economy fragile, amplifying competition for resources.

On the Saints' side, diplomacy and petitions sought redress. Delegations traveled to Jefferson City and Washington, D.C., to appeal for federal intervention. Their requests highlighted a tension in American federalism: the national government hesitated to override state jurisdiction, leaving vulnerable groups without remedy. The Liberty Jail letter therefore addressed a community disillusioned with secular institutions. D&C 121's promise that God sees and will judge becomes a theological answer when civil systems fail.

5. Geographic Context

Liberty Jail stood in Clay County's seat, Liberty, perched near the Missouri River. The jail's proximity to major waterways facilitated guard rotations but complicated rescue attempts. Far West—once the Church's headquarters—lay about 30 miles north. After the siege, Far West was largely abandoned, with Saints ordered to leave by spring 1839. Quincy, Illinois, the new gathering point, resided across the Mississippi River approximately 200 miles east. The physical distance between Joseph and the migrating Saints heightened communication delays: letters required trusted couriers traveling dangerous routes.

The jail's architecture shaped the revelation's metaphor. Walls several feet thick blocked sunlight, inspiring imagery of "pavilions" and "hiding places." A fireplace smoked constantly, blackening the ceiling. Straw beds on the stone floor rarely dried out in winter humidity. Joseph's descriptions of "hellish" conditions were more than figurative—they mirrored bodily suffering. Yet the close quarters also fostered communal devotion: prisoners prayed in unison, sang hymns, and recorded visions. The geographical confinement became a crucible for collective revelation.

6. Timeline of Events

  • October 25, 1838: Battle of Crooked River results in militia mobilization against the Saints.
  • October 27, 1838: Governor Boggs issues Executive Order 44, authorizing the extermination or expulsion of Latter-day Saints.
  • October 30, 1838: Haun's Mill massacre occurs.
  • October 31, 1838: Joseph Smith and leaders surrender at Far West, expecting protection.
  • November 1-3, 1838: Courts-martial at Far West; General Samuel Lucas sentences Joseph and others to death, but General Alexander Doniphan refuses to carry out the illegal order.
  • November 13-28, 1838: Prisoners endure harsh treatment en route to Richmond and Liberty. Parley P. Pratt and others are held in Richmond jail.
  • December 1, 1838: Joseph and companions confined in Liberty Jail.
  • January 1839: Emma Smith visits Liberty Jail; reports of attempted poisonings surface.
  • February 1839: Sidney Rigdon released on bail due to illness; Joseph begins drafting extended letters.
  • March 20, 1839: Joseph writes the extended epistle that becomes D&C 121-123.
  • April 6, 1839: The Twelve Apostles (defying threats) meet at Far West in obedience to earlier command to depart on a mission.
  • April 6-16, 1839: Prisoners transferred from Liberty under the pretext of moving to Boone County for trial. Guards allow them to "escape" near Yellow Creek, Missouri—likely a tacit arrangement recognizing flimsy charges.
  • April 22, 1839: Joseph reunites with the Saints in Quincy, Illinois.
  • May 1839: Saints begin gathering to Commerce, Illinois (later Nauvoo).
  • July 1839: Selected excerpts from the Liberty Jail letter published in the Times and Seasons, spreading the revelation widely.

Introduction

This verse-by-verse study provides complete scripture text, doctrinal commentary, linguistic insights, cross-references, and reflection questions for each segment of D&C 121.

Scripture Text

Doctrine & Covenants 121:1 — "O God, where art thou? And where is the pavilion that covereth thy hiding place?" Doctrine & Covenants 121:2 — "How long shall thy hand be stayed, and thine eye, yea thy pure eye, behold from the eternal heavens the wrongs of thy people and of thy servants, and thine ear be penetrated with their cries?" Doctrine & Covenants 121:3 — "Yea, O Lord, how long shall they suffer these wrongs and unlawful oppressions, before thine heart shall be softened toward them, and thy bowels be moved with compassion toward them?" Doctrine & Covenants 121:4 — "O Lord God Almighty, maker of heaven, earth, and seas, and of all things that in them are, and who controllest and subjectest the devil, and the dark and benighted dominion of Sheol—stretch forth thy hand; let thine eye pierce; let thy pavilion be taken up; let thy hiding place no longer be covered; let thine ear be inclined; let thine heart be softened, and thy bowels moved with compassion toward us." Doctrine & Covenants 121:5 — "Let thine anger be kindled against our enemies; and, in the fury of thine heart, with thy sword avenge us of our wrongs." Doctrine & Covenants 121:6 — "Remember thy suffering saints, O our God; and thy servants will rejoice in thy name forever."

Doctrinal Summary

Joseph's opening plea models sacred lament. Rather than masking anguish, he brings his distress before God, echoing the Psalms. The prophet acknowledges God's omnipotence while wondering why deliverance tarries. The language of "pavilion" and "hiding place" conveys Joseph's perception that heaven is veiled, not absent. By recounting God's power over creation and Sheol, Joseph asserts faith even as he questions timing. True discipleship permits honest petitions without forsaking reverence.

The cry for justice recognizes that righteous anger can coexist with charity. Joseph longs for God to "stretch forth" His hand and avenge the oppressed, yet he frames vengeance as belonging to the Lord. Verse 6 pivots from imprecation to praise: if God will remember His suffering Saints, they will rejoice forever. Lament thus becomes an act of covenant loyalty—trusting that God hears and will act.

Language & Cultural Insights

  • sukkâh (סֻכָּה) — "pavilion, booth." Joseph draws on Hebrew imagery where God's presence is described as a pavilion shielding His glory (Psalm 27:5). The plea for the pavilion to be lifted requests renewed revelatory access.
  • racham (רָחַם) — "to have compassion." In verse 3, Joseph asks that God's "bowels be moved" with compassion, reflecting Hebrew idiom where the womb (racham) symbolizes tender mercy.
  • Sheol — transliterated Hebrew term for the underworld. Joseph's reference bridges ancient Israelite cosmology with Restoration understanding of spiritual realms.
  • Cultural note: Ancient petitioners often reminded deity of His attributes as part of covenant prayer. Joseph employs the same structure, demonstrating immersion in biblical patterns.

Cross-References

  • Psalm 13:1-2 — David's refrain "How long, O LORD?" parallels Joseph's repeated question.
  • Isaiah 63:15 — Isaiah asks God to look down from heaven and recognize Israel's suffering, matching Joseph's appeal to divine sight.
  • Doctrine & Covenants 109:49-50 — The Kirtland Temple dedicatory prayer similarly pleads for deliverance from enemies.

Scripture Text

Doctrine & Covenants 121:7 — "My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment;" Doctrine & Covenants 121:8 — "And then, if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high; thou shalt triumph over all thy foes." Doctrine & Covenants 121:9 — "Thy friends do stand by thee, and they shall hail thee again with warm hearts and friendly hands." Doctrine & Covenants 121:10 — "Thou art not yet as Job; thy friends do not contend against thee, neither charge thee with transgression, as they did Job."

Doctrinal Summary

The Lord responds tenderly, calling Joseph "My son." Divine peace does not erase affliction but reframes it as brief compared to eternal reward. Exaltation is promised conditionally—"if thou endure it well"—highlighting agency amid suffering. Verses 9-10 remind Joseph of earthly support: true friends remain loyal, unlike Job's companions. The Lord simultaneously consoles and calibrates Joseph's perspective, acknowledging pain while orienting him toward ultimate triumph.

These verses demonstrate God's personal awareness. He knows Joseph's relationships, future reunions, and emotional needs. Afflictions become "a small moment" not because they are trivial, but because God's eternal timeline dwarfs temporal agony. The Lord invites Joseph to measure his story against Job's archetypal endurance, assuring him he has not been abandoned by his community or God.

Language & Cultural Insights

  • eirênê (εἰρήνη) — Greek for "peace." New Testament usage links peace with Christ's calming presence (John 14:27); the Lord extends that same covenantal peace to Joseph.
  • hypomonē (ὑπομονή) — "Patient endurance." The phrase "endure it well" reflects this virtue central to early Christian discipleship.
  • Cultural note: Comparing Joseph to Job situates him within the wisdom tradition, suggesting that righteous suffering refines faith and yields divine commendation.

Cross-References

  • Doctrine & Covenants 121:7-8 cross-reference with Doctrine & Covenants 122:7-9 — both promise sanctifying experience.
  • James 5:11 — Job's patience is celebrated, connecting Joseph's comparison.
  • Doctrine & Covenants 98:1-3 — Saints are told to give thanks in tribulations because God avenges their wrongs.

Scripture Text

Doctrine & Covenants 121:11 — "And they who do charge thee with transgression, their hope shall be blasted, and their prospects shall melt away as the hoar frost melteth before the burning rays of the rising sun;" Doctrine & Covenants 121:12 — "And also that God hath set his hand and seal to change the times and seasons, and to blind their minds, that they may not understand his marvelous workings; that he may prove them also and take them in their own craftiness;" Doctrine & Covenants 121:13 — "Also because their hearts are corrupted, and the things which they are willing to bring upon others, and love to have others suffer, may come upon themselves to the very uttermost;" Doctrine & Covenants 121:14 — "That they may be disappointed also, and their hopes may be cut off;" Doctrine & Covenants 121:15 — "And not many years hence, that they and their posterity shall be swept from under heaven, saith God, that not one of them is left to stand by the wall."

Doctrinal Summary

God pledges that false accusers will see their schemes evaporate like morning frost. The Lord has "set his hand" to advance His purposes; persecutors who resist are blinded to His marvelous works. Divine justice is reciprocal: those who delight in others' suffering will experience the consequences they engineered. The sweeping language of verse 15 warns of generational impacts—when families perpetuate violence, they risk collective downfall.

This passage reassures the Saints that God's justice may be delayed but not denied. Rather than commanding the Saints to retaliate, the Lord promises to expose craftiness and overturn oppression. Joseph learns that opposition cannot frustrate God's timetable; those who attempt to halt Zion's progress will ultimately fail. The imagery anchors hope in God's sovereignty over history.

Language & Cultural Insights

  • kairos (καιρός) — "appointed time." The Lord references "times and seasons," resonating with New Testament language about God's ordained moments (Acts 1:7).
  • panourgia (πανουργία) — "craftiness, unscrupulous cunning." Paul uses the term in 1 Corinthians 3:19; the Lord echoes Paul's assurance that God catches the wise in their craftiness.
  • Cultural note: Ancient covenant law of retaliation (lex talionis) emphasized measured justice. Here, God adapts the principle spiritually: the harm persecutors plot rebounds upon them under divine supervision.

Cross-References

  • 1 Corinthians 3:19-20 — Paul states that God catches the wise in their craftiness, matching verse 12.
  • Proverbs 26:27 — He that digs a pit shall fall into it, paralleling divine reciprocity.
  • Doctrine & Covenants 3:1-3 — God's work cannot be frustrated, reinforcing that opposition will fail.

Scripture Text

Doctrine & Covenants 121:16 — "Cursed are all those that shall lift up the heel against mine anointed, saith the Lord, and cry they have sinned when they have not sinned before me, saith the Lord, but have done that which was meet in mine eyes, and which I commanded them." Doctrine & Covenants 121:17 — "But those who cry transgression do it because they are the servants of sin, and are the children of disobedience themselves." Doctrine & Covenants 121:18 — "And those who swear falsely against my servants, that they might bring them into bondage and death—" Doctrine & Covenants 121:19 — "Wo unto them; because they have offended my little ones they shall be severed from the ordinances of mine house." Doctrine & Covenants 121:20 — "Their basket shall not be full, their houses and their barns shall perish, and they themselves shall be despised by those that flattered them." Doctrine & Covenants 121:21 — "They shall not have right to the priesthood, nor their posterity after them from generation to generation."

Doctrinal Summary

God condemns those who falsely accuse His anointed servants. Lifting up the heel evokes betrayal, reminiscent of Psalm 41:9. Accusers project their own sinfulness onto the righteous. Such slanderers risk excommunication from sacred ordinances, loss of temporal prosperity, and forfeiture of priesthood authority. The penalty extends generationally when posterity inherits traditions of rebellion.

These verses warn that opposing God's servants carries spiritual peril. The Lord defends agency yet emphasizes accountability. Those who weaponize legal systems to destroy the innocent sever themselves from the blessings they once enjoyed. The repetition of "saith the Lord" underscores divine certainty—these are not Joseph's retaliatory wishes but God's pronouncements.

Language & Cultural Insights

  • māšîaḥ (מָשִׁיחַ) — "anointed one." Though D&C uses English, the biblical concept frames called leaders as consecrated by God.
  • pseudomartyreō (ψευδομαρτυρέω) — "to bear false witness." Christ condemned such acts in Matthew 19:18; Joseph's persecutors exemplified it.
  • Agriculture imagery (basket, barns) reflects covenant curses listed in Deuteronomy 28, suggesting divine withdrawal of prosperity from the wicked.

Cross-References

Introduction

D&C 121 introduces vocabulary that defines righteous leadership and covenant resilience. The terms analyzed below appear in the revelation's climactic discourse (verses 26-46) and its opening lament. Each entry follows the mandated six-layer framework: Hebrew and Greek roots (via Blue Letter Bible), Latin development (Logeion), English etymology (Etymonline), Webster's 1828 definition, and doctrinal significance within the Restoration. Twelve terms are presented to provide comprehensive coverage of the section's core concepts.

Greek Analysis

Latin Analysis

sacerdotium — Latin for priesthood, highlighting holy office (sacer = sacred) plus agentive suffix -dotium (act of giving or endowment).

Etymology

Webster 1828

Priesthood — "The office or character of a priest; the order of men set apart for sacred offices." Webster notes its application to both Aaronic and Melchizedek orders.

Doctrinal Significance

D&C 121 teaches that priesthood rights are inseparably connected to heavenly power (verses 36-37). Priesthood is less a status than a conduit for God's authority, contingent on righteousness, charity, and avoidance of compulsion.

  • Create hand motions for the attributes in verse 41 and practice during singing time.

Visual & Hands-On Activities

  • Liberty Jail Diorama: Build a small jail using blocks or containers; place paper figures of Joseph and his companions inside while discussing God's answers.
  • Dew Drop Blessings: Spray plants with water as children list ways God sends knowledge like dew.

Object Lessons

  • Flashlight Pavilion: Cover a flashlight with cloth to show how prayers invite light to pierce darkness.
  • Scepter of Righteousness: Craft paper scepters labeled "Kindness," "Love," "Truth" and discuss how true power comes from these qualities.

Crafts & Take-Home

  • Create bookmarks with D&C 121:45 illustrated. Encourage children to tell their families about keeping thoughts virtuous.
  • Provide a "Peace Jar" where children add paper drops each time they feel the Holy Ghost bring comfort.

Teaching Tips

  • Use role-play (e.g., correcting a friend kindly) to practice verse 43 principles at age-appropriate levels.
  • Emphasize that God hears children's prayers just as He heard Joseph's.

7. Mission Applications

Teaching Principles

  • Share verse 7 when investigators face trials; testify that Christ offers peace amid adversity.
  • Use verses 41-46 to describe how church leadership differs from worldly authority, countering misconceptions about hierarchy.
  • Explain verses 26-33 to present Restoration theology of continuing revelation and councils in heaven.

Finding & Member Work

  • Invite members to identify friends experiencing "small moment" trials and accompany missionaries in ministering visits.
  • Develop lessons where members recount modern "knowledge poured down" stories, strengthening investigator faith in living prophets.

Companion Unity & Leadership

  • Companions review verses 41-43 weekly, evaluating how persuasion and love guide companionship councils.
  • Mission leaders incorporate D&C 121 in zone conferences, modeling correction followed by love to maintain unity.
  • Encourage missionaries to keep a Liberty Jail gratitude list—documenting blessings received during difficult areas.

Follow-Up Strategies

  • Assign investigators to read D&C 121:45-46 and report how they feel when inviting the Spirit into their homes.
  • Utilize the millstone imagery (verse 22) carefully to explain God's protection of the innocent and His justice against persecutors.
