Understanding the patterns: Mummies, Witches, Pirates, and Divine Convergence

Central Message: The papyri weren't just ancient curiosities—they were divine catalysts that unlocked the temple ordinances and priesthood understanding we have today.

In July 1835, while the Latter-day Saints struggled with bankruptcy from building the Kirtland Temple, Joseph Smith made what seemed like a financially reckless decision: he spent $2,400 (roughly $150,000 today) to purchase four Egyptian mummies and two papyrus scrolls from a traveling showman.

But these weren't just museum pieces. Wrapped in linen beside these ancient bodies were scrolls that would become the catalyst for temple ordinances, priesthood understanding, and covenant theology as we know them today.

Dr. Kerry Muhlestein, a respected Egyptologist and professor at BYU, traces the papyri's journey from Thebes, Egypt across the Mediterranean, over the Atlantic Ocean, through American exhibition halls, to Joseph Smith's home in Kirtland. The environmental challenges alone make their survival miraculous.

When Jean-François Champollion—the scholar who deciphered the Rosetta Stone—opened ancient Egyptian papyri in Turin, Italy in 1828, they disintegrated upon exposure to the Mediterranean climate. Yet Joseph's papyri crossed an ocean, toured America in multiple climates, and survived.

Muhlestein notes that he spent two weeks in Kirtland and was rained on 10 of those 14 days. He spent over a year in Egypt and was rained on twice. The humidity difference is staggering. The fact that these scrolls survived their journey to one of the dampest cities in America is, as Muhlestein says, "environmentally obnoxious"—and miraculous.

"It's a miracle truly that they survived environmentally. The difference in humidity is incredible." — Dr. Kerry Muhlestein

It's remarkable to consider that through a period when the Saints were in such transition, moving from one place to another, they were carting along 4 mummies! Picture the logistics: fleeing persecution in Kirtland, traveling to Missouri, then to Illinois—all while transporting ancient Egyptian mummies and fragile papyri. Here's where it gets profound. Dr. Muhlestein has written extensively about how ancient Egyptians intentionally imitated the original Abrahamic temple worship. They knew about Abraham—his name appears frequently in Egyptian religious texts from 200 BC to 700 AD, often interchangeable with Osiris in funerary texts.

We must remember that Egypt became a major center for the Jewish community after the Babylonian exile. Jeremiah went there (Jeremiah 43). Jewish temples were built there—most notably at Elephantine and Leontopolis. The city of Alexandria had an enormous Jewish presence, with scholars estimating the Jewish population at 100,000 to 200,000 people—roughly one-third of the city's total population. Two of Alexandria's five districts were predominantly Jewish. In Thebes (where the papyri originated), Jews formed a significant community as well.

As a polytheistic society, the Egyptians were quick to absorb and adopt Israelite traditions. They incorporated biblical figures—especially Abraham, Moses, and Jehovah—into their religious texts, often using them interchangeably with their own deities.

The irony? Pharaoh's descendants tried to copy what their ancestor rejected (Abraham 1:27). They couldn't hold real priesthood, but they imitated the forms for thousands of years. And in God's wisdom, their imitations help us to better understand the originals.

One of the reasons Dr. Muhlestein says he enjoys Egyptology so much is that he can write about Egyptian temples in ways he can't publicly in relation to LDS temple practices. He can go into specific details about temple traditions and symbols, many of which share connections to LDS and Jewish traditions. By studying what the Egyptians preserved (though corrupted over time through their natural man drift), we come to understand our own temple worship better.

What is Ontology?

Ontology asks the fundamental question: "What does it mean to exist? What makes something what it is?"

In philosophy, ontology studies:

  • The nature of being itself
  • What it means to be a certain kind of being
  • How beings can change from one state to another
  • Whether beings have essential qualities that define them

When Joseph worked on the Egyptian Alphabet and Grammar, he wasn't just translating words. He was exploring how covenant ordinances transform the very nature of human existence—how mortals become gods, how telestial beings become celestial beings, how natural men become spiritual beings.

Central Message: Gospel light transforms places of historical darkness—no past is too dark for redemption, no city too haunted for souls to be saved.

On August 6, 1836—just months after the Kirtland Temple's glorious dedication—Joseph Smith stood in Salem, Massachusetts, a city infamous for one of America's darkest chapters: the witch trials of 1692-1693.

He wasn't there for revelation. He was there seeking buried treasure.

It was a desperate measure during desperate times. The temple had drained Church finances. Debts were mounting. A man named Jonathan Burgess had told Joseph about gold supposedly hidden in a Salem cellar—riches that could solve all their problems.

So Joseph, Sidney Rigdon, Hyrum Smith, and Oliver Cowdery made the 600-mile journey to Salem, walking the same streets where, 144 years earlier, innocent people had been hanged as "witches."

The irony was profound: A prophet of God seeking temporal treasure in a city built on spiritual darkness, while back in Kirtland, ancient prophetic scrolls containing Abraham's revelations sat in the temple translation room.

But the Lord had other plans. Salem's real treasure wasn't buried gold—it was buried souls waiting for gospel light.

To understand why Salem is the perfect "witch" in our Halloween tale, you need to know its dark history.

Winter 1692 - It began with children's fits. Nine-year-old Betty Parris and eleven-year-old Abigail Williams started having violent convulsions, screaming, throwing things, contorting their bodies in "unnatural" positions, and speaking words that seemed blasphemous.

The village doctor, unable to find a physical cause, declared they were under "the Devil's hand." The witch hunt began.

The Accusations Spread:

  • The girls accused Tituba (a slave from Barbados), Sarah Good (a homeless beggar), and Sarah Osborne (an elderly woman who rarely attended church)
  • Under interrogation (and likely torture), Tituba confessed to witchcraft and claimed a conspiracy of witches in Salem
  • This confession triggered mass hysteria
  • More than 200 people were accused over the next year
  • Accusations spread like wildfire: neighbors accused neighbors, children accused parents, the powerful accused the weak

The Trials:

  • "Spectral evidence" was allowed (testimony that an accused person's spirit appeared in dreams or visions)
  • This meant anyone could accuse anyone based on nothing but claims of supernatural visions
  • No defense was effective—denial proved guilt (witches lie), confession proved guilt (you admitted it)
  • 19 people were hanged
  • 1 man was pressed to death (heavy stones placed on his chest until he died)
  • 5 people died in prison awaiting trial

The Victims:

  • Rebecca Nurse (71 years old, deeply religious, widely respected—didn't matter)
  • George Burroughs (former minister who recited the Lord's Prayer perfectly at the gallows—traditionally believed impossible for a witch, but he was hanged anyway)
  • John Proctor (tried to defend his wife, was accused himself and executed)
  • Giles Corey (pressed to death after refusing to enter a plea; his last words: "More weight")

What Made Salem's Witch Trials Particularly Dark:

  1. Community Betrayal - Neighbors testified against neighbors, children against parents, friends against friends
  2. Religious Justification - Done in God's name, by church leaders, with prayer and scripture
  3. Legal Authority - Courts, judges, official proceedings gave evil a veneer of legitimacy
  4. Spectral Evidence - Impossible to disprove; anyone could be accused based on someone else's dream
  5. Mass Hysteria - Fear feeding fear, suspicion becoming certainty, paranoia becoming policy
  6. The Innocent Died - People who refused to lie, who maintained their integrity, were killed for it

By 1836, Salem carried this haunting legacy—a city built on fear masquerading as faith, accusation masquerading as righteousness, murder masquerading as justice.
